The fig jam that named the house

Hannah14 June 20266 min read

It started, as these things do, with a tree we couldn't keep up with. The old fig in the corner of the garden produces more than two people and five guest rooms could ever eat — so the first summer, in self-defence, I started making jam.

By the second summer the jam had a queue. Guests asked for jars to take home; the café down the road asked to stock it; and somewhere in there the house got its name. We'd been calling it "the parsonage" out of habit, but a guest signed the book "thank you for the marmalade house" and that, as they say, was that.

It isn't really marmalade

Pedants, look away: it's a fig jam with a little orange in it, which is close enough for a name and far enough for the lawyers. The orange is the trick — a whole one, skin and all, sliced thin and cooked down until it goes to jelly. It cuts the sweetness of the figs and gives the whole thing a grown-up edge.

A hotel can fake a lot of things. It can't fake a good breakfast — and it certainly can't fake the jam.

Hannah's fig & orange jam

Makes about six jars. You'll want ripe figs — the squashy ones the birds have already found are perfect.

  • 1.5 kg ripe figs, stalks off, quartered
  • 1 whole orange, sliced wafer-thin, pips out
  • 900 g sugar, and the juice of a lemon
  • A splash of water and an hour of your morning

Combine everything in your widest pot, leave it an hour to get to know itself, then cook gently until the figs collapse and a spoonful wrinkles on a cold plate. Jar it hot. Eat it on our bread, ideally on our stoep — but yours will do.

Come down early, before the street wakes up, and take the window seat. There's always a jar open.
