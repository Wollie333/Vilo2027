Twelve thousand hectares

Naledi Mokoena · Co-founder28 May 20269 min read

When my father bought the first three thousand hectares of NenGama in 2009, the land was exhausted. Overgrazed, fenced into squares, its wildlife long gone. The estate agent called it a farm. He had a different word for it.

He called it a promise. Not because of what it was — bare red earth, a few stands of tired acacia, the silence of a place stripped of almost everything — but because of what it could become if we simply stopped using it and let it rest. The idea was almost embarrassingly simple: take the internal fences down, remove the cattle, and let the bush decide what it wanted to be. The hard part, it turned out, was the patience.

Rewilding is not a dramatic act. There is no single morning when the wilderness returns. It comes back in small instalments, so slowly that you only notice in hindsight. First the grass, thickening season by season until the soil stopped washing away in the summer storms. Then the grazers — duiker, then kudu, then a nervous herd of zebra that arrived one winter and decided to stay.

The fences come down

Between 2011 and 2019 we bought the neighbouring farms one at a time, as families sold up and moved on, and each time the first thing we did was take down the fence between us. It sounds trivial. It is anything but. A fence line is a wound across a landscape — it stops the seasonal movements that grazers have followed for millennia, concentrates pressure on the wrong places, and turns one living system into a dozen struggling ones.

"You don't build a wilderness. You remove the things stopping it from returning, and then you wait."— Naledi Mokoena

By the time the last fence came down, those scattered squares had become one unbroken system of twelve thousand hectares. And with space restored, the animals that need space could finally return. We reintroduced rhino under armed protection. The plains game multiplied. And then, on their own, following the herds, came the predators — first brown hyena, then leopard, and finally, on a cold July morning in 2020, the first lion any of us had seen on this land in living memory.

Why it stays small

People sometimes ask why we built only three suites on twelve thousand hectares. The honest answer is that the lodge exists to fund the land, not the other way around. Every booking pays for the anti-poaching unit, the rhino monitoring, the boreholes that keep the waterholes alive through the dry months, and the forty people from neighbouring villages who make the whole thing run.

  • A full-time anti-poaching team patrols the reserve, day and night.
  • Our rhino are monitored and notched, their movements logged daily.
  • Boreholes feed the waterholes through the worst of the dry season.

Keep the lodge small and the maths works: fewer guests, lighter footprints, a bigger share of every rand going back into the ground. It is not the fastest way to run a business. It is, we think, the only honest way to run this one.

My father didn't live to see the lions arrive. But he was right about the promise. The land kept it — we just had to get out of the way.
