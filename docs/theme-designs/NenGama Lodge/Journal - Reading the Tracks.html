Reading the tracks

Tebogo Modise · Head Tracker12 June 20267 min read

Before the sun has cleared the ridge, while the grass is still grey with dew, Tebogo is already crouched in the sand at the side of the track — reading the night's news.

The first thing he teaches you is to stop looking at the bush and start looking at the ground. We have driven barely two hundred metres from the lodge when he raises a hand and steps down from the vehicle, and there, pressed into the soft red sand, is a story I would have driven straight over: a wide pug mark, four toes, no claws. "Leopard," he says, without ceremony. "Female. She came through maybe two hours ago, walking, not hunting." He can tell from the depth, the spacing, the way the sand has begun to dry at the edges.

For Tebogo, the reserve is not a landscape so much as a ledger — a record constantly being written and erased. Every animal that moves through the night leaves a line in it, and a good tracker reads those lines the way the rest of us read a page. Who passed. How fast. How long ago. Whether they were calm or afraid.

The grammar of the sand

There is a grammar to it, he explains, and like any language it takes years to read fluently. A walking animal places its back foot neatly into the print of its front. A running one throws sand. A nervous one stops and turns. The spacing between prints tells you size; the sharpness tells you time. By the time we have followed the leopard's line for twenty minutes, I am beginning — barely — to see what he sees.

"The animals are always here. Tracking just teaches you to notice that they were."— Tebogo Modise

What strikes me most is how little of it is about the dramatic find. We do not, in the end, see the leopard — and Tebogo does not seem to mind. The point, he says, was never only to catch up to her. The point is the reading itself: the slow, attentive walk; the hundred small signs most guests would never notice; the way three hours on foot rearranges how you see everything afterward.

What the bush asks of you

By mid-morning the sand has gone hard and bright and the tracks have all but vanished. We turn back toward the lodge for coffee. I ask Tebogo what he wishes more visitors understood, and he thinks about it for a long moment.

  • That patience is the whole skill — the bush rewards the people who slow down.
  • That an empty drive is never empty; you simply weren't reading it.
  • That the best sightings are often the quietest ones.

We came home without a single big cat on the camera. And yet I have rarely felt so completely inside a place. That, I think, is what Tebogo has been trying to show me all morning — not an animal, but a way of paying attention.

TM
